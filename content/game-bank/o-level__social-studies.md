## TEACHING

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Q1 Inference and Message"
what: "Say what the source is telling you about the issue, going beyond what it literally shows. The message is the point of view the author wants you to take away."
how: "State the message in one sentence ('Source A suggests that...'), then quote or point to the specific detail that carries it ('shown by the words...' / 'shown by the figure of...'). Give a second supported inference if the tariff is 4 marks or more. Never simply describe: description without a point of view stays at the lowest level."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Q1 Message of a Cartoon"
what: "Read a visual source the way you read a written one. Every label, caption, exaggeration, size difference and facial expression in a cartoon is a deliberate signal."
how: "Work through four checks: (1) Who or what does each figure or object REPRESENT? (2) What is being exaggerated, and what does the exaggeration imply? (3) What does the caption or label add? (4) Therefore, what is the cartoonist saying about the issue? Write the answer as message first, evidence second. Do not slide into explaining WHY it was drawn: that is the purpose question."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Purpose"
what: "Explain why the author produced the source: what they wanted the audience to think, feel or do. Purpose is about intention, not content."
how: "Use the provenance first: who made it, when, for whom, at what moment in the debate. Then state the intended effect with an 'in order to' phrase: 'in order to persuade readers that...', 'in order to warn the public that...', 'in order to rally support for...'. Finish by tying the intention back to a detail in the source that was chosen to produce that effect."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Q2 Comparison"
what: "Compare two sources by CONTENT and by MESSAGE, and answer 'how far' by giving both agreement and disagreement."
how: "Pair the points: 'Source A says X, and Source B agrees because it says Y.' Then 'However, Source A implies P while Source B implies the opposite, Q.' Quote from BOTH sources in every pair. Then compare the overall messages, not just surface details, and end with a supported 'how far' verdict: mostly agree, mostly disagree, or agree on this but differ on that. Two separate summaries with no pairing is the commonest way to lose these marks."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Reliability and Provenance"
what: "Judge how far a source can be trusted as evidence. This is a judgement about the AUTHOR and the circumstances, not about whether the content sounds convincing."
how: "Run the provenance habit: WHO wrote it (and were they in a position to know?), WHEN (before, during or after the event, and does that matter?), WHY (do they have an interest in presenting it this way?), and TO WHOM (would the audience have shaped what was said?). Then cross-reference: do other sources corroborate or contradict it? Conclude with a measured verdict, and remember that a one-sided source can still be reliable evidence OF that side's view."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Q3 Utility and Usefulness"
what: "Judge how useful a source is FOR THE STATED PURPOSE in the question. Usefulness is never asked in the abstract."
how: "Answer in three moves. (1) Content: what does the source actually tell me about the exact thing asked? Quote it. (2) Provenance: does who made it, when and why make that content more or less valuable, and does the source's own bias make it useful as evidence of a viewpoint? (3) Limits: what does it NOT tell me, and what would I still need? Never dismiss a source as useless because it is biased: state what it IS useful for."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Cross-Referencing"
what: "Test any claim against the other sources and your own knowledge instead of treating each source in isolation. This is the engine of the reliability, surprise and assertion questions."
how: "For each claim ask: which other source supports this, and which contradicts it? Name them ('Source C corroborates this because...', 'but Source E contradicts it because...'). Where sources clash, explain the clash using provenance: different authors, different moments, different audiences, different interests. Then say what you can safely conclude from the set as a whole."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Q4 Surprise"
what: "Answer 'Are you surprised by this source?' by testing an expectation, not by describing the source. Surprise only exists relative to something you already believed."
how: "Set up the expectation first: 'Source E led me to expect that...' or 'From my own knowledge I would expect that...'. Then test it: 'Source D shows the opposite / the same, because...'. Then explain WHY, using provenance: a different author, a different date or a different audience often explains why two sources point different ways. Give a clear verdict, and be willing to say you are surprised in one respect and not in another."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Q5 The 10-Mark Assertion"
what: "'Using the sources, explain how far you would agree with this statement.' Every source must be used, and own knowledge must be brought in. It is the most predictable question on the paper."
how: "Group, then evaluate. (1) Group the sources that SUPPORT the statement and explain how each supports it, quoting. (2) Group the sources that CHALLENGE it and do the same. (3) Evaluate as you go: weigh each group by provenance and by cross-referencing, so you are judging the sources, not just listing them. (4) Reach a justified conclusion on HOW FAR you agree, explaining why the sources you found stronger outweigh the others. Bring in own knowledge to explain, corroborate or qualify. Do not leave any source unused."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Section B Q6 Reasoned Argument"
what: "The 7-mark structured-response question: a reasoned argument or recommendation, set in the Singapore context, usually asking for two reasons, ways, benefits or consequences."
how: "Give TWO reasons and develop each fully using point, evidence, explanation, link. Point: state the reason. Evidence: give a concrete Singapore-relevant example or fact. Explanation: show HOW the reason produces the outcome, step by step. Link: return to the exact words of the question. One reason explained well plus one asserted reason stops at the middle level; two developed reasons in the Singapore context reaches the top."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Section B Q7 Two-Sided Judgement"
what: "The 8-mark question: two factors or two perspectives, plus a judgement ('how far do you agree', 'which is more important', 'more X or Y')."
how: "Explain factor or perspective one with evidence. Explain factor or perspective two with evidence. Then JUDGE: state which is more important or how far you agree, and justify it with a criterion, for example which affects more people, which lasts longer, which is the root cause, or which the other depends on. A stand that is asserted but not justified sits below a stand that is argued. Never leave the judgement to a one-line final sentence with no reason."
```
