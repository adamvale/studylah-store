## TEACHING

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Inference"
what: "Work out what a source SUGGESTS beyond what it plainly says - the point of view behind the words or the image."
how: "State the inference in one sentence, then support it with a short quotation or a described detail from the source. Never simply copy or paraphrase the source: 'This source suggests that... I know this because it says...'"
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Comparison"
what: "Compare two sources by CONTENT and by overall MESSAGE, and say how far they agree."
how: "Never describe one source and then the other. Pair the points: 'Source A says X, and Source B agrees because it says Y.' Then do the same for the differences. Finish by judging whether the sources agree on the surface but differ in their message, or the reverse."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Purpose and message"
what: "Explain WHY the source was produced, not just what it shows - the effect its author wanted on a chosen audience."
how: "Use the formula message + audience + intended effect + context. 'The message is... The author aimed this at... in order to make them... He did this because at the time...' Use the caption for date and author, and anchor the purpose in what was happening then."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Reliability and provenance"
what: "Judge how far a source can be trusted as evidence, using who made it, when, why, and for whom."
how: "Do not write 'it is biased so it is unreliable'. Explain WHICH part is unreliable and WHY the author had a reason to shape it that way, then check the claim against another source. A source can be unreliable as fact and still be reliable evidence of an attitude."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Utility (usefulness)"
what: "Judge how useful a source is FOR A NAMED ENQUIRY - usefulness always depends on the question being asked."
how: "Answer in three moves: what the content tells you about the enquiry; what the provenance adds (a one-sided source is strong evidence of attitudes); and what the source does not tell you, so it must be used alongside others. Never conclude that a source is useless."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Cross-referencing"
what: "Test one source against another - does Source A support, extend or contradict Source B?"
how: "Quote the exact claim in A and the exact claim in B that meet or clash. Say plainly whether B corroborates A or undermines it. Then weigh provenance to decide which is more convincing, and bring in your own knowledge to break the tie."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Surprise"
what: "Say whether a source is surprising by measuring it against YOUR OWN KNOWLEDGE and the other sources."
how: "Always answer both ways: 'I am not surprised because I know that...' AND 'but I am surprised because I would have expected...'. The marks come from the historical knowledge you use as the yardstick, and from provenance - who said this, and would you expect them to say it?"
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Grouping the sources"
what: "Before the part (e) answer, sort the sources into those that SUPPORT the assertion and those that do NOT."
how: "Write a two-column plan in rough: FOR the statement / AGAINST it. A source may sit in both columns if parts of it cut different ways. Then write in groups, not source-by-source: 'Sources A, C and E support the view because...' This structure alone lifts an answer out of the lower levels."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "The assertion answer (part e)"
what: "The final sub-question is always: 'statement... How far do these sources support this view?' It carries the most marks and needs ALL the sources plus your own knowledge."
how: "Group the sources for and against; evaluate at least one source on each side using provenance (do not just quote content); use your own contextual knowledge to test the sources; then give an explicit judgement on HOW FAR the set supports the view - fully, partly, or hardly at all - with a reason."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Using contextual knowledge"
what: "Own knowledge is what turns source description into source evaluation - it is required, not optional, in the judgement sub-questions."
how: "Use knowledge in three ways: to explain a reference in the source; to test whether the source is accurate or one-sided; and to fill a gap the sources leave. Signal it clearly with 'From my own knowledge...' so the marker cannot miss it. Keep it short and relevant - it supports the source, it does not replace it."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "The essay shape (L1 to L5)"
what: "Section B essays are marked in levels: description sits at the bottom, one-sided explanation in the middle, balanced explanation higher, and a supported judgement at the top."
how: "Plan three paragraphs: the named factor, at least one other factor, then a judgement paragraph. Every paragraph is Point - Evidence - Explanation, and the explanation must link back to the question. Narrative or a list of facts, however accurate, cannot climb past the lower levels."
```

```yaml
kind: sbq
rung: "Balanced argument and judgement"
what: "'How far do you agree?' asks you to weigh the given statement against alternatives and then decide."
how: "Argue the case FOR the statement, argue the case AGAINST it, then judge - and the judgement must give a criterion ('the most important cause was X because without it the others would not have led to war'). Never end with 'both sides had good points'; that is a summary, not a judgement."
```
